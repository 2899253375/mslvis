Introduction

This paper addresses the compound generalization problem in object detection, focusing on the model’s adaptability to variations in both categories and styles—a challenge commonly encountered in real-world applications. To solve this issue, we introduce a new setting, Open-Scene Object Detection, which captures the dynamic changes in object categories and visual styles present in authentic scenarios. Within this setting, we propose TPDA (Test-time Prompt Dynamic Adjustment), an innovative approach that enhances the generalization capabilities of vision-language models (particularly CLIP) in open-scene object detection tasks through prompt adjustment. TPDA leverages style-agnostic category descriptions generated by large language models and dynamically adjusts prompts at test time based on the style information of the target image, enabling the model to adapt seamlessly to diverse real-world scenes. Furthermore, we have constructed a new benchmark dataset, MS-LVIS, based on the LVIS v1.0 validation set. MS-LVIS includes 46,949 images, covering 18 complex real-world scenes and 1,203 categories, providing a comprehensive dataset for evaluating open-scene object detection. This paper not only offers a practical solution for compound generalization but also establishes a new benchmark, aimed at advancing research in open-scene object detection.

Characteristics

Benchmark Characteristics. MS-LVIS introduces a series of challenging settings designed to rigorously assess the limitations of object detection models in open-world scenarios.

Variability in Object Size and Aspect Ratios. MS-LVIS encompasses objects of various sizes and shapes, requiring models to recognize and distinguish objects under diverse visual conditions.

Complex Backgrounds and Overlapping Objects. Images in MS-LVIS often feature intricate backgrounds and overlapping objects, reflecting the chaotic environments surrounding objects in real-world (open-world) settings.

Multi-Category, Multi-Object Scenes. Each image in MS-LVIS may contain multiple objects from different categories, evaluating the model's robustness in both object classification and localization.

Long-Tailed Category Distribution. The object categories in the real world follow a long-tailed distribution, demanding models to exhibit strong capabilities in localizing and distinguishing rare or tail-class samples.

Cross-Domain Variability. Since MS-LVIS shares categories with LVIS, it can be combined with LVIS v1.0 to further challenge the generalization ability of OSD models across 18 different scenes.

These carefully crafted challenges are designed to simulate complex real-world conditions, providing a comprehensive benchmark to advance the development of OSD technologies.
